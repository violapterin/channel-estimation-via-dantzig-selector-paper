{
\I DS works for real matrices, but we generalize by representing a complex number by its real- and imarinary-parts.

\I DS works for sparse vectors, but \m {\M {H}} is not sparse.
We claim that \m {\M {K}^\Adj \M {H} \M {K}} is almost sparse.
Call the largest-magnitude \m {S} components of \m {\V{g}} to be \m {\M {g} _{\SB{\MC {A}}}}, and call the remaining vector \m {\M {g} _{\SB{\MC {C}}}}, we have to bound \m {\VNm {\M {g} _{\SB{\MC {C}}}} _1}.

\Frame {Modifying the Original Proof (2/2)}
{
\I DS requires the measurement matrix (in our case) \m {P} to have restricted isometry property.
We shall design all four beamformers, \m {\M {F}_B, \M {F}_R, \M {W}_B, \M {W}_R}, so that for high probability, they satisfies RIP, thus \m {\m {P}} does too.

\I We know the Big-\m {\MC {O}} of error norm of DS depends on (in our case) \m {N_H}.
We consider these failure events above, and claim that the algorithm works for high probability.
}

\Frame {Design of Beamformers}
{
\I Let us set each entry of \m {\m {F}_B} (resp.\ \m {\m {W}_B}) to be i.i.d.\ Complex Standard Normal, up to a design constant

\I And set each entry of \m {\m {F}_R} (resp.\ \m {\m {W}_R}) be uniformly distributed argument and unity magnitude, up to a design constant

\I It is argued in the treatise that \m {\M {F} _R ^\Tr}, \m {\M {F} _B ^\Tr}, \m {\M {W} _R}, and \m {\M {W} _B} all have RIP, then \m {\M {P}} has too.
